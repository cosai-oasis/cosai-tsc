### Meeting Notes – Based on AI-generated Summary

* Meeting Introduction and ACTION Institute Overview:   
  * Giovanni Vigna provided an overview of the ACTION Institute, noting that it has been operational for nearly three years as an NSF AI institute focused on agent-based cyber threat intelligence and operations. The institute is a $20 million joint investment from the National Science Foundation, DHS, and IBM, designed to foster research at the intersection of AI and security to protect mission-critical infrastructure.  
  * The institute’s central mission is to develop intelligent, agent-based security applications capable of assessing infrastructure, detecting attacks, and responding automatically. Giovanni highlighted that the institute envisions a future where intelligent agents collaborate with humans to enhance security and educate the next generation of the AI workforce.  
  * A key goal of the institute is to transfer research techniques, data sets, and approaches to commercial partners. Notable examples of technology transfer include CV Genie, a tool for the automated reproduction of vulnerabilities, which is currently being utilized by major companies.   
  * The ACTION Institute is a collaborative effort involving researchers from 13 universities, governed by an executive council to maintain focus and direction. The institute is supported by an evaluation team that provides feedback based on hard data and an external advisory board.  
  * Research is organized around four security thrusts: assessment, detection, attribution, and response and recovery. Giovanni noted a strategic adjustment regarding attribution, finding that AI-driven, proactive threat intelligence sharing—allowing similar targets to prepare for attacks—is a more critical research area than traditional attacker attribution.  
* **Foundational AI Research Approach:**   
  * The institute utilizes a "stack" approach to AI research, focusing on capabilities such as reasoning, knowledge representation, and human-agent interaction. Giovanni explained that the challenge lies in bridging the gap between general foundational AI research and the specific, practical system-level tasks required to secure infrastructure.  
  * To maintain focus across its research areas, the ACTION Institute utilizes mini-proposals that require collaboration between principal investigators at different institutions, ensuring measurable advancements. These efforts align with the broader landscape of the 29 national AI institutes, with the ACTION Institute specifically focusing on network security.  
* **GATE Infrastructure:**  
  * The institute developed the Global Accessible Test Environment (GATE), a platform that deploys the developed AI stack to allow researchers to test multi-agent security applications. This infrastructure, built on tools like Terraform and Ansible, has been used to simulate attacks on cyber-physical infrastructure.  
* **Educational Initiatives and Gamification:**   
  * Education is prioritized through new curricula on distributed learning and AI security. The institute actively utilizes gamification, including the development of AI-focused capture the flag (CTF) competitions, to engage high school and undergraduate students in creative and competitive environments.  
  * To facilitate the creation of autonomous agents without requiring advanced coding skills, the institute developed Agentish, a graphical framework.  This tool enables students to design multi-step agent workflows—including router, choice, and worker nodes—using natural language prompts and JSON data transmission.

* **Q\&A Section**  
  * Focus Areas and Industry Role: During the Q\&A, Giovanni clarified that the institute’s primary focus is "AI for security" and emphasized that they are not in the business of product development. While they aim to empower commercial entities, they do not engage in driving standards or regulations, noting that organizations like CoSAI are better positioned for that role.  
  * Threat Intelligence Sharing and Privacy: Discussing intelligence sharing, Giovanni noted that the institute is researching the balance between utility and privacy. They are developing agents capable of automating the sanitization of sensitive data, allowing organizations to share actionable threat intelligence without leaking private or infrastructure-specific information.  
  * Operationalizing Research for Industry: Jason and Josiah discussed the potential for operationalizing this research within specific industries, such as oil and gas. Giovanni noted that academic and industry collaboration can create a pipeline where algorithms developed by academics are tested and validated in real-world environments, serving as a pilot for industrial adoption.  
* **Closing Remarks on Collaboration:**  
  *  J.R. concluded the meeting by emphasizing the need to connect academic research assets—such as the Agentish framework and research papers—with industrial applications. The discussion highlighted a mutual interest in scaling these academic innovations to help enterprises solve pressing security challenges.  
  * Wenke Lee raised a concern regarding the future of academic research, noting that students often struggle to identify research topics that will not become obsolete within six months due to rapid advancements by frontier model companies. He cited prompt engineering and software bug analysis as specific examples, questioning the utility of academic research in areas where industry leaders are already significantly ahead of any potential publication. Wenke asked for industry perspectives, specifically inquiring whether organizations like IBM foresee a future where a limited number of dominant frontier models dictate infrastructure, leaving researchers to define their role in that ecosystem.  
  * J.R. acknowledged the widespread struggle to align academic and industry research goals, noting that while the achievements of frontier model companies are impressive, a significant portion of the technology's application remains unexplored. J.R. argued that the future lies in how this technology is implemented in enterprise AI, suggesting that universities are uniquely positioned to address needs regarding compliance, reliability, and validation, which are critical for enterprise adoption. J.R. noted that even with highly capable models, there is still substantial work required in areas such as software validation and the development of remediation tools.
