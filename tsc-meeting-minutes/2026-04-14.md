### Meeting Summary

* **Administrative Items and Blog Posts for Review:**   
  * Claudia introduced two blog posts for the TSC to review and vote on for consensus,. One is a blog post based on the presentation given by J.R. and Akila at RSAC, and the other is an announcement post for the Agentic Identity paper. An email with a call for consensus will be sent to the mailing list, with the review period running until Thursday.  
* **Potential NIST Collaboration on NIS Collab and ARMF Profiles:**  
  * David L. updated the group on conversations with Raymond from NIST regarding their shift in approach toward the ARMF profiles, emphasizing pointing to current ecosystem work rather than exclusively producing everything themselves. NIST is interested in CoSAI current and future development to influence this changing approach and identify areas for collaboration, particularly focusing on critical infrastructure, OT/ICS, and IT. Josiah and Daniel agreed that collaboration with NIST would be beneficial for testing and operationalization, provided that CoSAI is not slowed down by NIST’s natural cadence.  
  * Bill raised a question about looking into the OECD as an international synchronization group, in addition to standards bodies, noting that government relations work often starts there. David acknowledged that CoSAI is looking at broader global engagement, including in Asia and Europe, and emphasized that engagement with NIST is not mutually exclusive to other efforts, as NIST is primarily looking for best practices they can point to. Josiah suggested , specialized Special Interest Groups (SIGs) for each engagement to facilitate focused discussions and report back to the TSC.  
  * A key point of discussion was the necessity of ensuring that external entity engagement, such as with NIST, does not slow down CoSAI's ability to deliver timely, effective guidance. Zeal advised keeping technical framework engagement separate from high-level AI principles and policy engagement to prevent delays in operational guidance that practitioners urgently need. Daniel suggested that CoSAI should focus on the operational implementation gap, putting best practices to code, as NIST tends to focus on regulatory abstraction without providing specific implementation details.  
* **Collaboration with the UCSB Action Institute:**   
  * David L. provided an update on scheduling a meeting between UCSB Action Institute Principal Investigators (PIs) and CoSAI TSC and workstream leads, within the next four weeks. The initial meeting will involve each CoSAI workstream and aligned SIG presenting a 10-minute overview of their work, focus problems, and next steps, followed by a Q\&A session. The goal is to identify areas for collaborative work and help CoSAI increase its academic engagement. Josiah suggested creating a shared deck in the TSC Google Drive for all participants to insert their presentation pieces, which Claudia taking the action to provide the template deck.  
* **Shared Responsibility Framework SIG Update:**   
  * Shrey provided an update on the Shared Responsibility SIG, noting that version 7 of the white paper is available and requires review from the TSC. Before a secondary review, there is an action to update the personas in the paper to align with the CoSAI Risk Map (CoSAI-RM) personas for consistency across artifacts The team also needs to determine the next steps and deliverables following the framework and engage relevant participants from different workstreams.  
  * Josiah suggested that the momentum from the Shared Responsibility framework should be maintained to elaborate on governance activities, potentially by establishing a Governance SIG to connect with CoSAI-RM. Karttik suggested that the paper could be structured by AI offerings, as different engagement models exist for various products, such as APIs versus conversational chat agents. Karttik also offered to provide input on these differentiations as discussions continue.  
* **Secure AI-assisted Code (SecureCode) SIG Update:**  
  * Shrey reported that the SecureCode  SIG is progressing with discussions on adopting code guard rules and is requesting that everyone install and test these rules to provide feedback. Other activities include enhancing existing skills, such as adding a security reviewer skill that leverages best coding practices, which is being developed in both skill and sub-agent form factors. A new skill on memory-safe language migration is also in development.  
* **Security Agent SDLC (SecureADLC) SIG (Workstream 4\) Update:**   
  * Sarah updated the group on the SecureADLC SIG, noting that their next meeting is scheduled for tomorrow and they have two provisional leads from Red Hat and IBM but they are actively seeking more volunteers. The plan is to review the material presented at RSAC with the new leads, prioritize topics, and gauge the interest for subsequent work.  
* **Improving Onboarding and Discoverability:**   
  * David L. emphasized the need for the TSC to improve the discoverability of CoSAI's current properties, such as GitHub sites and websites, to make it easier for new individual members to onboard and come up to speed. This includes simple changes like making the meeting calendar easier to find, and potentially restructuring the CoSAI landing page on GitHub to prioritize engagement over other content. Daniel also raised the importance of improving the search engine optimization (SEO) of CoSAI content to ensure external traffic is routed upstream.  
* **Meeting Minutes and Governance:**  
  *  Sarah Novotny reported that WS 4 is exploring pulling more documentation into GitHub, including meeting notes, to enable AI-driven agenda building and content analysis. Claudia noted that while meeting notes for TSC and PGB are published on GitHub, cleaning up automated workstream notes requires significant effort due to inaccuracies, and any public sharing would require a formal review process, potentially stripping names for a Chatham House style summary. David L. stressed the need for a clear, written set of ground rules regarding the longevity and review of meeting content to ensure that participants understand the implications of their discussion.  
* **Discussion on Mythos, Glasswing, and CoSAI's Role:**   
  * The conversation shifted to the recent developments around Mythos and Glasswing, prompting a reevaluation of CoSAI's initial scope exclusion concerning securing frontier model generation and AI for attackers/defenders. David asked the TSC if there is an opportunity for CoSAI to engage with this moment, perhaps by leaning into problems closer to their core mission, such as providing rapid SDLC guidance for open-source maintainers, or addressing AI readiness for security programs. Daniel argued that the initial exclusion of AI for defense is unsustainable, as AI is becoming the engine for everything, and suggested CoSAI should focus on the net acceleration of AI patterns and the resulting impedance mismatches in the SDLC pipeline.  
* **The Impact of AI Acceleration on Open Source and Security:**   
  * The discussion highlighted the challenge of the accelerated rate of vulnerabilities and the lack of readiness in current SDLC pipelines, which results in humans having to manually fix thousands of bugs. Daniel pointed out the “OSS tax” problem: open source maintainers have zero tokens to spend on addressing AI-driven security problems, suggesting that a consortium-based ecosystem is necessary to fund this work. It was suggested that David L. should engage with Alpha-Omega and the Linux Foundation to gain insight into how CoSAI can help the open ecosystem with this problem.  
* **Blog Post Suggestion and Glasswing Outcomes:**  
  * Bill suggested a blog post focusing on how to handle Glasswing outcomes, specifically addressing the revealed vulnerabilities. The process would involve identifying those vulnerabilities, creating an inventory, comparing them, and resolving them as quickly as possible.   
  * Ian raised the question of whether smaller, cheaper models could replicate vulnerabilities, noting that they had been able to replicate the BSD  vulnerabilities. David L. countered that they knew where to look first, but Ian explained that the process involved mapping the software and triaging it, suggesting that one could spin off thousands of agents targeting different files.  
  * Daniel argued against eliminating CVEs entirely, stating that they were initially constructed for a human audience to transact information. They proposed that new channels are needed to go straight to agent remediation pipelines at scale, requiring an event-driven mechanism. The existing 90-day human-centric CVE model is becoming obsolete if remediation at scale needs to occur in less than 12 hours or an hour.  
  * David P. agreed with Daniel, emphasizing the need for asymmetric modes to adapt to the new paradigm, which currently appears to be an exponential hockey stick. David P. posited that the problem is one of combinatorics, and optimism stems from being able to normalize asymmetric modes such as looking for heuristics indicating that a pull request was driven by a bot.
