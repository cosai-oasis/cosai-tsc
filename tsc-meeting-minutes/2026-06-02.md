### Meeting Notes – Based on AI-generated summary

* **Introduction and Meeting Administrative Overview:**   
  * J.R. facilitated initial introductions for meeting attendees for this dedicated session to brief members of the [ACTION Institute](https://action.ucsb.edu/), including guests Giovanni Vigna, Wenke Lee, and Tim Robinson. 
  * J.R. provided a high-level overview of the CoSAI's purpose, noting that the meeting is the first of two sessions, with this session designed to introduce participants to the group's four primary workstreams and current objectives.  
* **CoSAI’s Mission:**  
  * David L. explained that the coalition aims to democratize the understanding of securing AI systems, focusing on technical security control invariants rather than general policy. The group emphasizes the need for collaboration between diverse entities—including large tech companies, researchers, and users with constrained resources—to build practical, research-backed guidance.  
* **WS1 \- Software Supply Chain Security – Overview:**   
  * Asmae outlined the workstream's focus on adapting existing software supply chain security concepts, such as SBOMs, for AI and machine learning systems. The team has produced papers on risk and controls and model signing, and are currently finalizing the AIM (AI Artifacts Integrity and Provenance) framework, which defines maturity levels for model signing. The group is seeking reference implementations to pressure test the framework and plan to address ongoing implementation challenges.  
  * Wenke initiated a discussion regarding whether the security industry is failing to apply historical lessons to new AI technologies, specifically questioning why AI agentic programs lack established protections like sandboxing or standardized review processes. J.R. and other participants responded that while established mechanisms like attestation and signing remain relevant, the primary challenge is determining which aspects of AI—such as new artifacts like data and models—require novel solutions versus the application of existing, proven methods.  
* **WS2 \- Defender Tools \- Overview:**  
  *  Josiah detailed WS2’s efforts to prepare defenders for a changing cyber landscape, noting that their work focuses on the unique aspects of AI, such as stochastic systems and data functioning as code.  After publishing the landscape paper on Preparing Defenders of AI Systems, the group released an AI Incident Response Framework, which provides playbooks and guidance for various incident types. Additionally, they developed an AI Shared Responsibility Framework that introduces five layers—including data and model provider layers—to ensure security requirements are operationalized and understood across different organizational roles.  
  * Giovanni inquired about how threat intelligence sharing maps to the AI Incident Response framework, specifically regarding how organizations should share information about attacker techniques. Josiah clarifies that the current framework touches upon the necessity of sharing information with government bodies and the broader community, suggested using standards like STIX and OpenCTI, and expressed interest in incorporating further contributions on this subject.  
* **WS3 \- AI Security Risk Governance \- Overview:**  
  * Shrey, Christina, and Zeal discussed WS3’s mission to provide practitioners with a common taxonomy for risk and controls. David L. explained the CoSAI Risk Map project, which aims to bridge the gap between abstract white papers and production environments by mapping guidance to actionable control and risk statements that can be consumed by existing risk libraries. Shrey added that the workstream is also focused on security for AI-assisted code development, including "Project CodeGuard" to provide rules and tools for secure coding practices.  
* **Workstream 4 \- Secure Design Patterns for Agentic Systems \- Overview:**   
  * Sarah and Ian introduced this workstream focused on agentic systems, noting that its charter was created to address the rapid rise of agentic AI. The group has published guidance on securing the Model Context Protocol (MCP) and is currently working on a second version that incorporates new protocols and grading models. The team is actively developing an RFC for agent credentials and exploring a trust-aware data plane to enhance control and audit capabilities in agentic workflows.  
  * Sarah and Ian also discussed the scope of the "agentic development life cycle" SIG under WS4, which seeks to identify necessary changes to the classical software development life cycle to support the creation, operation, and runtime of agents. The discussion indicated that the scope has become quite broad, potentially covering sandboxing and agent creation, so the group is working to narrow the focus to two main areas: the life cycle and runtime enforcement.  
  * Giovanni asked if the group has considered the security implications of using Large Language Models (LLMs) and agentic systems within real-world robotics or cyber-physical environments. Sarah clarified that while the topic has been discussed, they have not yet performed significant work in this area and have decided to table it until the group reaches a critical mass of resources.  
  * Giovanni asked about examples of "bad" design patterns and vulnerable agent applications, explaining that such data sets are critical for researchers to develop and test detection systems. Ian acknowledged the need for these resources and mentions that while they lack a single anchor point for this, they have been tracking internal GitHub issues and recently published a paper through IBM Research on internal analysis. The group discussed the potential to create a series of "vulnerable" agent examples.
