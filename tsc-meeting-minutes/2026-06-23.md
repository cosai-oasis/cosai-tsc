## Meeting Summary \- Based on AI Summary

* **Administrative Updates and Welcomes**: 

  * J.R. officially welcomed new committee member Courtenay from Microsoft and welcomed back David P.  following leave.

* **Year 3 Planning Framework**: 

  * J.R. and Akila initiated a discussion on planning for the third year of the Technical Steering Committee (TSC), emphasizing the need to reflect on the group's achievements over the past two years. Akila proposed using retrospectives to identify what worked and what did not, aiming to establish a strategic framework that focuses on efficiency gains and future technical priorities.

* **Proposed Strategic Technical Focus Areas**: 

  * The group discussed the importance of defining success criteria for the next six to twelve months, focusing on impactful technical goals rather than just process-oriented tasks. Akila sought input on which areas of the enterprise AI lifecycle—such as model development, usage, or inference—the group should prioritize to drive meaningful change.

  * ***AI Landscape and Security Gaps:***  Bill suggested adopting a holistic view of the AI landscape, including sectors outside the enterprise environment like aerospace and automotive, to identify current security coverage gaps. They expressed concern regarding the "meshed" AI environment where agents communicate and move data, noting that a lack of schema for permissions and autonomy thresholds creates significant security risks .

  * ***Trust Boundaries and SLA Metrics:*** David P.  advocated for focusing on trust boundaries by utilizing conformal prediction intervals to establish statistical guarantees for AI performance. By treating binary states as an SLA (Service Level Agreement) problem, the committee could theoretically govern AI behavior and prevent uncontrolled actions from autonomous agents.

  * ***Prioritizing Real-World Adoption:***  Josiah proposed that the group shift its focus toward achieving widespread adoption by building tools that customers can implement and provide feedback on. Akila and Josiah explored whether the current gap in adoption is due to a lack of marketing or the maturity of the tooling, with Josiah emphasizing that operationalizing best practices through tangible tools is a necessary next step.

  * ***Building a Sustainable Ecosystem:***  Sarah noted that successful open-source ecosystems require more than just industry thought leadership; they require a community of startups and users to build upon coalescing standards. They recommended that the committee prioritize bringing in partners who are willing to co-create and build businesses around the group's proofs of concept.

  * ***Developer Experience and Organic Growth:***  David P.  discussed the need to address common developer concerns, such as server-side state, to foster organic growth and adoption. They emphasized the importance of providing clear documentation to ensure that developers have a cohesive product they can stand behind.

  * ***Centralizing Product Management:***  Bill proposed that the TSC centralize the product management of their outputs, including white papers, to ensure consistent quality and impact. Akila questioned whether this effort should be centralized within the TSC or decentralized to workstream leads, with Bill suggesting that the high-level framework—defining products, audiences, and industry influence—remain centralized.

    * The group discussed the challenge of measuring the effectiveness of their output, with Bill noting that their primary indicator of impact has been engagement on social platforms. David P. highlighted the Model Context Protocol (MCP) threat categories as a core product that aligns with their mission, though they agreed that finding quantitative measures for adoption is a key challenge.

  * ***Maintenance and Sustainability of Tools:***  Sarah and Courtenay raised concerns about the risks of accepting tool contributions without a robust ecosystem to maintain them, noting that the committee must avoid creating "decaying" directories of outdated code. Courtenay proposed that a goal for the upcoming year should be to actively recruit dedicated maintainers to support the long-term viability of their tools.

  * ***Industry Maturity and Contribution Challenges:***  Sarah observed that the current industry climate tends to favor "not invented here" approaches, where companies prefer to build their own custom solutions rather than collaborate on shared standards. They emphasized that the committee must be judicious in selecting projects that have genuine community momentum.

  * ***Future Opportunities for Early-Career Talent**:* Sarah suggested that the committee provide opportunities for early-career individuals to participate in their work, which could bring new perspectives and drive the direction of the group. 

  * ***Capacity Constraints for Tooling**:* David L. voiced strong concerns against the committee's current capacity to sustain software tools, noting that without dedicated funding or full-time engineers, these projects risk becoming abandoned "graveyards" of code. David L. compared this to the model used by ML Commons, where resources are explicitly allocated to support the effort, and argued that the current volunteer-based model is insufficient for large-scale tool execution.

  * ***Shifting to Axioms and Blueprints**:* David P. and David L. discussed shifting the committee's focus toward developing axioms and decomposable blueprints rather than proprietary tools. David L. explained that decomposing white papers into actionable data structures, such as YAML or ontology-based models, allows the industry to leverage the group's knowledge without the committee needing to maintain extensive software repositories.

  * ***Coordination Versus Execution**:* The committee debated their role, with David L. and Josiah discussing whether the group should function as a coordinating body or an execution body. David L. noted that in some cases, the highest value is realized by pushing guidance into existing protocols, rather than building separate tools.

  * ***Implementing a Blueprint Model:*** Debadatta suggested that the committee create blueprints that external entities can adopt to develop their own industry-specific tools.  David L. supported this approach, suggesting that sector-specific collaboration could help materialize generalized guidance into actionable patterns for industries such as finance or telecommunications.

    * Debadatta emphasized that for the committee to remain viable, they must demonstrate a win-win situation for both participating companies and society, ensuring that the effort spent on contributions remains sustainable. They suggested an ecosystem model where the committee collaborates with partners to implement blueprints and receives recognition for their contributions.

  * ***Regional Workshops for Community Engagement**:* Josiah proposed hosting regional workshops to facilitate face-to-face interaction, which they believe would help accelerate community growth. David L. and the committee acknowledged this as an area for improvement, discussing ways to align these meetups with existing external meetings or governmental outreach efforts.

* **Logistics for Future Meetups**: Debadatta, J.R., and the committee discussed the possibility of initiating meetups in the New York area, with members offering their office spaces as potential venues. J.R. expressed support for organizing these informal gatherings to foster deeper collaboration, noting that the committee should coordinate these efforts after upcoming travel schedules.

* **Meeting Wrap-up**: J.R. concluded the meeting by thanking the participants for the free-form discussion regarding the third-year strategy. They reminded the committee that the insights gathered would inform their path forward and that they would reconvene the following week.
