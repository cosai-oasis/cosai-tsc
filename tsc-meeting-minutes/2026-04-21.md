## Meeting Summary \- Based on AI Summary

* **Administrative Updates and Publication News**: Ingrid provided the administrative update, announcing the publication of the Agentic Identity paper and blog, as well as the RSA blog written by Akila and J.R. Kelly mentioned the call for consensus for WS1 co-lead and the co-lead for the WS4 SecureADLC SIG is open until 6:00 p.m. EDT today, but they anticipate a positive outcome with no objections registered so far.

* **New Member Introduction**: Ingrid introduced Klaudia from Meta as a new member, who is temporarily replacing Lauren. 

* **Shared Responsibility Framework**: Akila Srinivasan introduced the Shared Responsibility Framework (SRF). Josiah explained that the SRF was identified as a top priority by the TSC, second only to CoSAI-RM, and aims to express dependencies that drive responsibility within AI solutions. Bill and Christopher were identified as the main authors, with contributions from many others.

* **Layered Model of the Shared Responsibility Framework (SRF)**: Bill presented the core concept of the SRF as a layered accountability framework that answers "who is responsible for what," using a five-layer model: business, information, application, platform, and model provider/supply chain. The criticality of the business process and its governance flows down through these layers, making everything below it important, while the model supply chain's responsibility includes training data and data sets. Service providers are typically not responsible for the customer's business processes, information, or the model provided by the supply chain.

* **SRF Comparison to Standard Cloud Models**: Bill compared the new SRF to the standard cloud shared responsibility model (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS) and the Azure AI model, noting that the standard cloud model addressed confusion over responsibility when moving to cloud services. The Azure AI model has only three layers (usage, application, platform), missing the critical business layer, the information layer, and the model supply chain, all of which are included in the new SRF. The SRF uses a chain of dependence from the use case down through the solution architecture.

* **SRF Personas and Operating Models**: Christopher emphasized that the SRF is an expansion beyond purely system implementation to include data and operational governance aspects, utilizing the five-layer model. The framework incorporates personas derived from the CoSAI RM, mapping them to the SRF layers to address shared responsibilities across different layers. The document also details four cloud operating models—AI Software as a Service, AI Platform as a Service, Agentic Platform as a Service, and Infrastructure as a Service—mapping them across the five responsibility layers, with specific details in Appendix A2.

* **Framework Application and Implementation**: The SRF structure includes an executive summary, current state challenges, the overall framework, and appendices that highlight its application across different environments and industries, including an implementation playbook example. Christopher noted that the framework is intended to establish practices and principles for accountability across all vendors and providers in a stack. Bill suggested that the layered approach can be used to apply models like the NIST Cyber Security Framework functions (Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover) or a five W's approach (Who, What, Where, When, Why) to assess the company layer by layer.

* **Addressing Heterogeneous Deployments and Cross-Border Issues**: In response to questions about heterogeneous deployment environments and cross-border business ecosystems, Josiah explained that the framework provides a method to decompose solutions and assign responsibility by parts, making it possible to create a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) for complex architectures. They agreed that cross-border, multi-jurisdictional governance issues would need to be considered for follow-on activity, potentially involving the risk workstream.

* **SRF Integration with Security and Compliance**: Raghuram asked how security controls and shared responsibility aspects of security overlay the stack, especially regarding issues like model guardrails or compromised prompts. Bill explained that security controls, such as the OWASP Top 10, can be mapped layer-by-layer, and this mapping is often dependent on the business segment and its regulations (e.g., FDA requirements for healthcare or SEC requirements for finance). The SRF aims to be complementary to existing frameworks like NIST AI RMF and ISO 42001, defining what governance outcomes need to achieve.

* **Continuous Maintenance and Future Tooling**: Jay commented that due to the fast pace of AI, the SRF will likely require continual maintenance and versioning, as it may not fully span every industry, particularly those with individual consumers subject to regional privacy laws. Josiah agreed, stating that this is the beginning of specifying the activities of governance for CoSAI, and that continual maintenance will be necessary. Karttik provided feedback, praising the paper's extensive coverage of use cases but expressed greater excitement for the potential "readiness gauging application" demoed by Bill, suggesting that a tooling output would be easier for consumers, such as small-to-medium businesses, to adopt as a baseline framework.

* **Next Steps for SRF Publication and Development**: Bill articulated the framework’s approval and publication readiness, and Josiah confirmed the intent to reboot the SRF SIG after publication to bring in new members and drive further development, including the specification of governance activities and controls. There was a suggestion to hand over the responsibility framework to WS3 to develop tooling, such as the readiness gauging application, and control packages tied to frameworks like NIST.
